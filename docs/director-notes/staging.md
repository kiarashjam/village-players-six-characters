# Staging

## The trap most productions fall into

They play it cool and clever. They emphasise the metatheatrical conceit, the breaking-of-the-fourth-wall, the cleverness of having actors play actors who can't act the characters.

That stuff is in the play, sure, but it's the frame, not the picture. Productions that lean into the cleverness produce an evening that audiences find interesting and respect and forget. Productions that play the family's wound — that take seriously the proposition that two children die and a man has done something he cannot undo and a woman has had her children taken from her — produce an evening audiences cannot get out of their heads.

The cleverness is what gets the play onto the stage. The wound is what justifies it being there.

## Why our staging choices are right (and one place I'd push further)

The minimalist black-box, the single defining visual per act, the silent presences of the Boy and Child as objects — these are all responses to the same underlying instinct: don't pretty it up. Don't decorate the wound. Trust the audience to imagine the things you can't show.

The two-level set in Act Two is the strongest of the three choices, I think — putting the actors-as-actors above and the Six waiting below makes the power structure of the play physical. The chair-circle in Act One does what an opening scene needs to do: it tells the audience this is going to be unadorned.

The fountain alone in Act Three is the one I'd push you on. There's a risk that *single short fountain basin alone on stage* reads, in the auditorium, as too tasteful — too aesthetic, too composed. Pirandello's Act Three is messier than that. You might want to keep the fountain but allow it to be slightly disorderly: water spilled around it, the chair-and-coat tipped over, the projection on the rear wall holding a beat longer than is comfortable. The composition should be slightly wrong by the end. The audience should feel that whatever this place is, it has had something happen in it.
