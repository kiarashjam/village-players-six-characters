# The play's central argument

Pirandello believes — or at least the play believes, which is different — that personality is a fiction we maintain together. Each one of us has within him a whole world of things and we cannot communicate that world to anyone else, ever; what we communicate is a thin agreed-upon version, the version polite society can hold, the version theatre can stage.

The Father's grand speeches about consciousness are not abstract philosophy. They are an accusation against language itself. *You — the audience — do not know me. Cannot know me. And I cannot make you.*

That's an unbearable thought. The play makes us bear it for two hours and then sends us home.
