# The play

## What it is, before anything else

It's a 1921 play about an interruption. A working theatre company is rehearsing, badly, a play they don't like — and is interrupted, in front of the audience, by six people in mourning who walk on stage and say: we are characters, we were started by an author, he abandoned us, please stage us before we vanish. The whole evening is the argument that follows. That's the entire play.

## What Pirandello did with that premise

What Pirandello did with that premise is harder than it looks. He didn't write a play *about* theatre — plays about theatre are a dime a dozen and most of them are smug. He wrote a play that breaks itself open in front of the audience and asks whether anything called "drama" can hold a real wound. The Six don't want to be staged because they want fame; they want to be staged because their story is unfinished and unfinished is the worst thing a story can be. That's a religious idea inside a metatheatrical comedy.
