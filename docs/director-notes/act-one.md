# Act One

## The thing nobody quite says about Act One

Act One pretends to be exposition but isn't. The Father's long philosophical speeches look like he's explaining the family's situation. He's actually doing something else — he's defending himself, to anyone who'll listen, against an accusation nobody has formally made yet. By the time the Step-Daughter rips into him in the back half of the act, you realise the long monologues were the testimony of a man who knew the trial was coming.

Most productions play those speeches as philosophy. They land much better as defence.
