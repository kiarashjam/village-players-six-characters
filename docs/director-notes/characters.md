# Characters

Readings of the principal parts. Opinions, not blocking.

## The Step-Daughter

The Step-Daughter is the play's engine. Without her, the Father gets to keep talking and we'd all sit politely. She refuses to let him.

She is the only character who has fully understood that you cannot get justice from theatre, that theatre will always soften and prettify and turn pain into something an audience can comfortably watch — and yet she demands to be staged anyway, because not being staged is worse. That contradiction is the role.

An actor who plays her as just angry will exhaust the audience by Act Two. An actor who plays only the wound will lose the comedy. The trick of the part is that she's funny because she's furious; her sharpness is comedy and pain at the same beat. The Madame Pace shop scene works only if the audience laughs at her cynicism for the first thirty seconds, then realises what they were laughing at.

## The Mother

The Mother is the most misread role in the play. She has 36 speeches. People think that means she's a small role.

She is not a small role. She is the role whose silence makes the rest of the play possible — every philosophical statement the Father makes about reality and conscience is tested against the fact that this woman, sitting there, has lost her children and cannot say so in any language anyone wants to hear.

When she finally says *It's taking place now. It happens all the time*, it's not a line. It's the only direct testimony in the entire play. Everyone else is performing their suffering. She is the suffering.

Cast it wrong — cast for restraint, cast for delicacy, cast for "a quiet presence" — and you'll have an evening with a hole in the middle of it.

## The Son

The Son is the most modern character in the play. He's the one a contemporary audience recognises immediately, even before they can name what they recognise.

He refuses to participate in his own family's catastrophe. He won't be staged. He won't perform his pain. He looks at the rest of them and registers contempt. He is, essentially, every adult child who has been told they have to "show up" for a parent's narrative and has decided no.

The play uses him as a refusal mechanism — every time the production starts to congeal around the Six as a tragic unit, the Son's refusal cracks it open again. Without him, the play becomes sentimental. With him played correctly, it stays raw.

## The Manager

The Manager is the audience proxy and also the play's villain — both at once. He's the one we identify with at the start (he's just trying to do his job) and the one we resent by the end (he's lost a whole day and learned nothing).

Pirandello's trick is that the audience walks in aligned with the actors and ends up aligned with the Six. The pivot happens through the Manager. If the actor plays him as a buffoon, you lose the journey. He has to be smart enough that we trust him in Act One, exhausted enough that we feel for him in Act Two, and oblivious enough by Act Three that we realise he was never going to understand what we now understand.

The exit line *I've lost a whole day over these people* is one of the meanest closing lines in twentieth-century theatre because it tells you who the audience is. Pirandello is putting his arm around you in the dark and saying: you, in the seat, have just spent two hours with these people. Did you also lose a whole day?

## The Players

The Players (the working actors of the inner company) are not comic relief. Or rather, they are, but they're also the play's bitter joke.

They are competent professionals who have given their lives to a craft that, in the moment that matters, is shown to be entirely unequal to the reality presented to it. They cannot do what the Six need them to do. Not because they're bad actors — because no actor can. Theatre cannot represent the truth the Six carry.

The Players' working-actor griping (cook's caps, contracts, Stanislavski) is funny precisely because it is so much smaller than the situation in front of them. Played well, the audience laughs at them in Act One and feels for them by Act Three, because by then the audience has understood that the Players' inadequacy is everyone's inadequacy. We are all the Players. We can't quite stage the things that matter most either.

## The Boy and the Child

The Boy and the Child are the hardest pieces of the play to direct. They are also the two roles I'd argue Pirandello cares about most.

They are children carrying the consequences of adult catastrophe and they have no language for it. The Child drowns. The Boy shoots himself. These are not metaphors. The play insists they happen. And yet — they happen offstage, or behind scenery, or in a way that no production can show truthfully. Pirandello wrote a play that ends with two children dying and made it formally impossible to depict the dying.

The choice we made — the chair-and-coat, the wrapped bundle, the brief silent projections — isn't just a clever staging idea. It's the only honest answer to a problem the play sets you. The bodies of children are not theatrical material. The chair-and-coat says: we know that. We are not going to pretend we can do this. We are going to ask you to imagine it instead.

## Madame Pace

She's the play's most famous theatrical effect — the character who appears because the actors are "necessary" for her to materialise, conjured by stage logic rather than by writing. Most productions play this for spookiness or comedy. Both are right.

But the deeper thing about her is that she's the play's first piece of evidence that characters might be more real than people. She arrives because the situation requires her to. She speaks half-French half-Italian — a real Lausanne immigrant in our version — and she runs a shop, and she has done what she has done to the Step-Daughter, and none of that is invented because the play needed it; it's the way the world is.

The Madame Pace effect tells you something the play won't say out loud: the Six aren't fictional. They're more real than the Players, because they cannot be edited. They are what they are. The Players, by contrast, will go home after this rehearsal and forget all of it by Tuesday.
